How the AI learns

Genetic Algorithm

A genetic algorithm mimics natural selection. Instead of writing rules for the paddle, we create a population of random neural networks and let them compete.

  • Population: 16 neural networks, each with different weights.
  • Fitness: each network plays ping pong for a short round. Networks that hit the ball more often get higher fitness scores.
  • Selection: the best performers survive to the next generation.
  • Crossover and mutation: surviving networks combine their weights and add small random changes.

Over many generations the population gets better at keeping the ball in play.

Neural Network

Each paddle controller is a tiny neural network. It receives five inputs:

  • Ball horizontal position
  • Ball vertical position
  • Ball horizontal velocity
  • Ball vertical velocity
  • Current paddle position

The inputs flow through a hidden layer of neurons and produce one output: the direction and speed the paddle should move.

Why it fails first

Early generations move almost randomly. The ball often flies past the paddle. As fitter networks reproduce, the population learns patterns like tracking the ball and anticipating bounces. The failures are a normal part of learning.
